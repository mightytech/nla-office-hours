# From Focus Groups to Simulations: AI Stakeholder Perspectives for Local Governance

## The Idea

Most people hear "policy" and think of Congress. Legislation drafted over months, debated on the floor, enacted into law. That's policy at one scale.

Policy also happens when a town of 3,000 weighs whether to add speed bumps on a residential street. When the city council tries to figure out which roads to pave with a budget that can't cover all of them. When an urban school district with flagging enrollment decides which schools to close. When a local health department crafts messaging for a vaccination campaign and needs to reach communities that distrust the medical establishment.

These decisions affect people's lives directly. And the people making them — town managers, school board members, public health officials, planning commissioners — rarely have the resources to understand all the perspectives involved. A focus group costs money they don't have. A public comment period surfaces whoever showed up. A survey captures what people are willing to say in a form, which misses what they actually think.

The idea: use AI to simulate stakeholder perspectives on local policy questions. Not to replace public engagement — to supplement it. Surface concerns nobody thought to ask about. Identify affected groups that weren't in the room.

This isn't a vision for transforming federal governance. It's a tool a town manager could use next Tuesday.

---

## Why Local, Why Now

The federal policy imagination — AI helping Congress draft legislation, simulating the effects of tax reform — is real but distant. It requires institutional adoption, regulatory frameworks, political will, and trust-building at scale. It's a decade-out proposition at best.

Local governance is different:

**The resource gap is enormous.** A city council deciding which streets to repave doesn't have a policy analysis team. A school board weighing closures might hire one consultant if the budget allows. A health department crafting outreach has a communications person, maybe two. These are consequential decisions being made with almost no analytical support beyond the decision-makers' own experience and whoever shows up to the public meeting.

**The decisions are concrete.** Speed bumps, not tax policy. School closures, not education reform. Vaccination messaging, not healthcare legislation. The specificity makes simulation tractable — the stakeholders are identifiable, the effects are local and observable, the feedback loop between decision and outcome is short.

**The stakes are personal.** A federal policy affects people abstractly. A school closure affects the family on Elm Street whose kid now has a 45-minute bus ride. Road paving priority affects the neighborhood that's been waiting three years while the new subdivision got fresh asphalt. Vaccination messaging that misreads a community's concerns doesn't just fail — it deepens distrust. The people making these decisions feel the consequences in their own community.

**The barrier to trying is low.** A town manager doesn't need institutional approval to try a new analytical tool. A school superintendent can experiment before the next board meeting. A health department director can test messaging approaches before committing to a campaign. There's no procurement process, no regulatory review, no political risk in exploring perspectives before making a decision.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

### Speed Bumps on Maple Street

The town received complaints about speeding. The obvious solution: speed bumps. But who's affected?

The simulation surfaces perspectives:
- **The parent who filed the complaint** — wants the bumps, has kids who play near the road
- **The elderly resident with a bad back** — speed bumps make every trip painful, might avoid the route entirely, increasing isolation
- **The volunteer fire department** — response time on that street increases by 30-45 seconds per bump, could matter in an emergency
- **The property owners** — some research suggests speed bumps reduce property values slightly; others suggest the safety improvement increases them
- **The delivery driver** — reroutes to avoid bumps, shifting traffic to adjacent streets that now have the speeding problem
- **The teenager learning to drive** — the road is the only direct route to the high school; bumps create a new hazard for inexperienced drivers in winter

The town manager might have thought of two or three of these. The simulation surfaces all of them, with reasoning, before the decision is made. Not all perspectives are equally important — the fire department concern might be decisive, the delivery driver concern might be trivial. But now the decision-maker sees the landscape.

### Which Roads to Pave

Budget covers four miles. Eight miles need work. How do you choose?

Beyond traffic volume and road condition (which the town already knows), the simulation surfaces equity and access concerns:
- The low-income neighborhood whose residents can't afford the alignment damage from potholes
- The business district where road condition affects whether customers stop or drive to the next town
- The school bus routes where bad roads create safety risks for children
- The neighborhood that was passed over last cycle and the one before that — the cumulative signal of neglect
- The subdivision developer who contributed to the council member's campaign and whose new road "needs" repaving after one winter

Not all of these will surface in a public meeting. The equity concern might not have an advocate. The cumulative neglect might not occur to anyone looking at this year's data. The political dynamic will be obvious to insiders and invisible to the public. The simulation creates a fuller picture than the decision-makers' assumptions alone.

### School Closures

Enrollment is down 20%. Two schools need to close. Which ones?

The standard analysis: enrollment numbers, facility condition, geographic coverage. The simulation adds the human layer:
- **The family that moved to this neighborhood for this school** — they'll consider leaving the district entirely, further accelerating the enrollment decline the closure was supposed to address
- **The single parent who works nights** — the current school is walking distance; the receiving school requires a car or a bus that doesn't run at 6am
- **The special education community** — one of the closure candidates has the district's best SPED program; moving it "on paper" doesn't move the relationships, routines, and institutional knowledge that make it work
- **The community identity** — the school is the last public institution in that neighborhood; closing it is a signal about whose community matters
- **The teachers** — some will transfer, some will leave; the ones who leave are often the ones the district can least afford to lose

A school board member knows some of this from experience. The simulation surfaces what experience doesn't cover — the second-order effects, the perspectives of people who don't show up to board meetings, the cascading consequences that only become visible after it's too late to reverse the decision.

### Vaccination Messaging

The health department needs to increase vaccination rates in a community with low uptake. What messaging works?

The wrong approach (and the common one): craft a message about vaccine safety and efficacy, translate it into the community's language, distribute it through official channels. This fails because it addresses the health department's understanding of the problem, not the community's.

The simulation surfaces what the community actually thinks:
- **The immigrant family** — distrust isn't about the vaccine; it's about government contact. Any official outreach triggers fear of documentation status being checked
- **The religious community member** — the concern isn't theological; it's that the health department dismissed their questions last time instead of engaging genuinely
- **The young parent** — trusts their pediatrician completely but the pediatrician's office doesn't have the vaccine in stock; the "go to the county clinic" message means taking time off work, arranging childcare, and navigating a system designed for people with free afternoons
- **The conspiracy-adjacent neighbor** — doesn't believe 5G causes COVID but does believe pharmaceutical companies lie about side effects, because they have — repeatedly, documentedly. The message that pretends the concern is irrational alienates instead of engaging

The health department can't run four focus groups to surface these perspectives. They can use a simulation to draft messaging that addresses what people actually think, not what officials assume they think. Then test that messaging with real community members, using the simulation as a starting hypothesis rather than a final answer.

## What This Is and What It Isn't

**It is:** A way to surface perspectives, concerns, and second-order effects before making a decision. A supplement to existing processes. A tool that makes the decision-maker's understanding broader, not a replacement for their judgment.

**It isn't:** A substitute for actual public engagement. A way to skip the town meeting. An oracle that tells you what to do. The simulation doesn't vote, doesn't decide, and doesn't carry accountability. The human decides — always.

**The right mental model:** Preparation, not process. The town manager runs a simulation before the meeting, not instead of the meeting. The school board uses it to anticipate concerns, not to justify a conclusion they've already reached. The health department tests messaging approaches in simulation, then validates with real people.

**The quality depends on the archetypes.** A simulation with flat stereotypes — "retired homeowner," "young family," "business owner" — produces predictable responses that confirm what you already assume. That's not a simulation worth running; it's ChatGPT role-play with extra steps. The value is in complexity, contradiction, and specificity. The elderly resident who supports the speed bumps in theory but can't physically tolerate them. The immigrant family that wants vaccination but fears the system that provides it. The teacher who loves the school but knows it should close. The surprise is where the insight lives. Flat personas generate agreement. Rich archetypes generate tension — and tension is what surfaces the things you didn't think to ask about.

## The Hybrid Model Applied

This is the hybrid model in its clearest form. The AI generates perspectives and reasons about second-order effects. Traditional analysis provides the hard numbers — budget data, enrollment figures, road condition assessments, geographic calculations, demographic data. The human weighs it all, makes the decision, and bears the accountability.

But the most important thing the human brings isn't judgment in the abstract — it's local knowledge. The simulation doesn't know that the fire chief and the town manager are brothers-in-law, or that the last school closure nearly cost the superintendent her job, or that the neighborhood association president is a former state legislator who will turn a local decision into a regional news story. The human knows. The simulation gives them a broader landscape to apply that knowledge to.

## Why This Matters Beyond the Tool

This isn't really about AI. It's about who gets analytical support for their decisions.

A member of Congress has the Congressional Research Service, committee staff, lobbyists competing to provide analysis, think tanks producing white papers, and a budget for consultants. A town manager in a community of 3,000 has Google and their own experience.

The gap between the analytical resources available at the federal level and the local level is enormous — and it's the local decisions that affect people most directly. Whether the school stays open. Whether the road gets fixed. Whether the vaccination campaign actually reaches the people who need it. Whether the speed bumps create more problems than they solve.

If an AI tool can give a town manager even a fraction of the analytical breadth that a congressional staffer takes for granted — surfacing perspectives they hadn't considered, identifying affected groups they didn't think of, generating the uncomfortable questions before the public meeting instead of during it — that's not a vision for transforming governance. It's a practical tool for making local government slightly less blind to the consequences of its own decisions.

The people most affected by policy have the least access to policy analysis. That's the gap. Not Congress. Not legislation. The town meeting where nobody thought to ask the fire department about response times.

## Open Questions

- **Archetype quality.** The value of the simulation depends entirely on the quality of the perspectives generated. Flat stereotypes produce useless results. How do you generate stakeholder perspectives that carry genuine complexity? The archetype extraction approach (drawing from literary nonfiction like Studs Terkel's *Working*) is a promising direction, but untested for local governance contexts.

- **Validation.** How do you know the simulation surfaced real concerns? The only test is whether real stakeholders, consulted afterward, say "yes, that's what I actually worry about." This is testable — take a real municipal decision, run the simulation beforehand, then compare to what emerges in the actual public process.

- **Trust and adoption.** Local officials are practical people. They'll try a tool if it helps them do their job better. They won't try it if it sounds like academic theory or Silicon Valley hype. The presentation matters: "here's a way to prepare for the next board meeting" works; "AI-powered stakeholder simulation platform" doesn't.

- **The scope question.** This focuses on local governance, but the same pattern applies anywhere consequential decisions are made by people without analytical staff: nonprofit boards, neighborhood associations, community health organizations.
